What Happens During Pet Cremation? An Honest Guide for Newcastle Families

20 Jul 2026 — For many families, the cremation process itself is a mystery. This guide answers the questions people often feel awkward asking, from preparation to ash return.

Before the Cremation: How Your Pet Is Prepared

When your companion arrives — whether collected from your home, your vet, or brought by you — they are treated with the same dignity and respect you would expect for any family member. Here is what happens before the cremation begins:

  • Identification is attached. A traceable ID tag stays with your pet throughout the entire process. This ensures the ashes you receive are yours and yours alone — a protocol reputable crematoria follow rigorously.
  • Non-organic items are removed. Collars, harnesses, tags, and any medical devices (such as catheters or bandages) are taken off. You can request these back if you wish.
  • The fur stays. Dogs and cats are not shaved before cremation. The coat is left intact as a natural covering. If you would like a fur clipping as a keepsake, this can be arranged — just ask.
  • They are placed gently. Your pet is laid in a dignified position within the cremation chamber. Nothing is rushed.

During the Cremation: What Actually Happens

The cremation chamber reaches a carefully controlled high temperature — typically between 800°C and 1,000°C. Here is what happens, step by step:

  1. Soft tissues are reduced by the intense heat. This includes organs, muscle, and fur. The process is self-sustaining once it begins and is contained within a sealed, ventilated chamber.
  2. Bones and teeth remain. These are primarily calcium phosphate — a mineral that does not burn. The heat makes the bones brittle, but the skeletal structure survives the cremation largely intact at this stage.
  3. The duration varies by size. A small pet like a cat or rabbit typically takes 1–2 hours. A medium dog may take 2–3 hours. A large-breed dog may take 3–4 hours. These are typical ranges — every cremation is conducted individually and time is not a constraint.

Can I Watch My Pet Being Cremated?

Yes — at many crematoria, including many serving Newcastle families, witnessed (or attended) cremations are available. You can be present as the process begins, often after spending time in a private farewell room. This is entirely optional and must be arranged in advance. Some families find comfort in witnessing the start of the process; others prefer to say goodbye beforehand and leave the rest to us. There is no right or wrong way.

Does Pet Cremation Smell?

This is one of the most common questions families ask but rarely voice. The answer is reassuring: the process produces very little odour. The chamber is sealed, and modern cremation equipment is designed with emission controls. The ashes you receive are processed bone fragments — they are composed mostly of calcium phosphate and have little to no detectable smell. Families consistently report that their pet's ashes are odourless.

After the Cremation: What You Receive Back

Once the cremation is complete and the chamber has cooled, the bone fragments are collected. Here is what happens next:

  • The fragments are processed. A machine called a cremulator reduces the brittle bone fragments into a fine, sand-like consistency. This is what families receive as "ashes."
  • The chamber is thoroughly cleaned between every individual cremation. Staff sweep and vacuum to ensure every particle is collected. This is part of the traceability protocol that ensures you receive only your pet's ashes.
  • Any metal is removed. Surgical implants, if present, are separated after the cremation and disposed of appropriately.
  • The ashes are placed in your chosen container — typically a scatter tube or hand-finished casket — and returned to you along with a certificate of individual cremation.

Do You Get All the Ashes Back?

This depends on the type of cremation you choose:

  • Individual (private) cremation: Yes. Your pet is cremated alone. Every effort is made to return all of their ashes to you. Read our full guide to communal vs individual cremation.
  • Communal cremation: No ashes are returned. Multiple pets are cremated together and the combined ashes are typically scattered in a memorial garden. This is a dignified option for families who do not wish to keep the ashes.

What to Do With Your Pet Before Cremation

If your pet has passed at home and collection is a few hours away, here is how to care for them:

  1. Place a towel underneath to catch any fluids.
  2. Wrap them in a clean blanket. Arrange the body in a natural, curled position if possible — this is easiest within the first 1–2 hours before rigor mortis sets in fully.
  3. Keep them cool. For small pets, a refrigerator set just above freezing works well. For larger animals, place ice packs around the wrapped body (not directly on it) and keep them in the coolest room available. A garage or utility room is often suitable.
  4. Contact us promptly. Complete our contact form and we will arrange collection — typically the same day, at a time that works for you.

There is no rigid timeline you must follow. Take the time you need to say goodbye. We will never rush you.

Common Questions Families Ask

Here are honest answers to the questions we hear most often from Newcastle families:

How soon after death should cremation happen?

There is no legal minimum waiting period for pet cremation in the UK. Cremation can happen as soon as the paperwork is complete and collection is arranged. Some families prefer same-day collection; others need a day or two. Both are fine. The participating provider works around your needs.

Is it better to cremate or bury my pet?

This is a personal choice. Home burial is legal in the UK on your own property, provided the grave is deep enough (at least 1.25 metres) and away from water sources. However, cremation offers flexibility — you can keep the ashes with you, scatter them somewhere meaningful, or take them if you move house. For many families, cremation provides closure and portability that burial does not. See our memorial products page for ways to honour your companion.

What memorial options are available?

We offer hand-finished wooden caskets and urns, scatter tubes, paw-print keepsakes, memorial jewellery, and engraved plaques. Each is chosen to help you remember your companion in a way that feels right. Browse the memorial products described here for reference or read our memorial services guide for more detail.

How much does pet cremation cost?

Costs vary by your pet's size and the type of cremation you choose. Individual cremation typically costs more than communal because it involves dedicated chamber time and ash return. For a full breakdown without specific £ amounts (which change), see our 2026 pet cremation costs guide or pricing page.
